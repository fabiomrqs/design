Context

As Spring's product portfolio continued to grow, multiple squads were designing and shipping features simultaneously across Sales, Logistics, Finance, and Operations.

While this enabled rapid product expansion, it also introduced a growing challenge: inconsistent experiences, duplicated work, and increasing effort to design and build new products.

As Staff Product Designer, I led the creation of a Design System to establish a shared foundation that could scale across teams, products, and engineering.

The Challenge

At first, the issues appeared unrelated.

Different products used different UI patterns. Similar components behaved differently. Designers recreated existing solutions, while Engineering maintained multiple implementations of the same interface.

After mapping the ecosystem, it became clear that these inconsistencies were symptoms of a much larger problem.

The challenge wasn't only about creating consistent interfaces. It was creating a scalable way to build products.

Research

To understand where the friction existed, I audited our design files, component libraries, and existing products, while working closely with Product Designers and Engineers.

Key findings

  • Similar problems were solved differently across products
  • Teams spent time recreating components instead of solving new problems
  • Design handoffs required constant clarification
  • Engineering duplicated implementation effort
  • There was no single source of truth shared across teams

Building the Vision

Rather than creating another UI kit, I proposed building a product infrastructure shared by Design and Engineering.

The vision was guided by four principles:

  • One shared design language
  • Reusable by default
  • Documentation that supports decisions
  • Built to scale across products and teams

Building the Foundation

The Design System started with the foundations: design tokens, typography, spacing, colors, iconography, accessibility, and interaction principles.

From there, I designed a scalable library of reusable components with documented behaviors, variants, and usage guidelines.

To reduce implementation effort, I also developed the production-ready component library, allowing Engineering teams to integrate approved components directly into the platform instead of rebuilding them from design specifications.

AI as Part of the System

As AI became part of our product workflow, I expanded the Design System beyond people.

I created structured Markdown documentation containing design principles, component behavior, accessibility guidelines, UX writing standards, and interaction patterns.

These files became the knowledge base used by our internal UX agent in Claude, allowing AI to generate interfaces and design recommendations aligned with the Design System.

Instead of documenting only for designers and engineers, the system was also designed for AI.

Driving Adoption

A Design System only creates value when teams choose to use it.

I worked closely with Design and Engineering throughout implementation, refining components, documentation, and workflows as new products adopted the system.

The combination of Figma libraries, production-ready components, and AI documentation made the Design System the fastest path for building new features.

Results

The Design System became the shared foundation for product development across Spring's platform.

Key outcomes

  • Established a single design language across multiple products
  • Reduced design and development time through reusable foundations
  • Eliminated most traditional design handoff by delivering production-ready components for Engineering integration
  • Reduced implementation costs by minimizing duplicated engineering effort
  • Improved collaboration between Product Design and Engineering through a shared source of truth
  • Enabled AI-assisted product design through structured Markdown documentation consumed by our internal UX agent
  • Created a scalable foundation for future products, teams, and AI-powered workflows

Key Learnings

This project reinforced that modern Design Systems are no longer just libraries of components. They're shared product infrastructure.

By connecting Design, Engineering, and AI through the same source of truth, we reduced delivery effort, improved consistency, and created a foundation that scales with both teams and technology.
