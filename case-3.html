Context

As Staff Product Designer at Spring Global, I led the design of Coach AI, an AI-powered assistant created to help Sales Managers make faster and more confident decisions.

Spring's platform supports retail distribution operations for global brands such as Coca-Cola, Unilever, and Red Bull. Every day, Sales Managers monitor sales performance, plan field activities, and respond to operational issues through a centralized dashboard.

While the dashboard provided extensive operational data, it wasn't helping managers make decisions efficiently.

The Challenge

The existing dashboard provided a comprehensive view of sales performance, but investigating issues remained a slow and manual process.

Operational analytics revealed a consistent pattern.

Data insights

  • More than 12 interactions were required, on average, to investigate a single issue
  • The average response time to sales issues exceeded 4 hours
  • Only 35% of performance drops were addressed within the same day

To understand why these inefficiencies existed, I conducted field research with Sales Managers.

Research

Through field interviews and journey mapping, I observed how managers investigated performance issues and made operational decisions.

Key findings

  • Managers understood what was happening
  • Identifying why it was happening was time-consuming
  • Deciding what to do next lacked confidence
  • There was little visibility into whether their actions would produce the expected outcome

The problem wasn't access to data, it was supporting better decisions.

Building the Vision

To better understand what an effective decision-support experience should provide, I mapped the complete decision-making journey.

Every investigation followed the same sequence of questions.

01. Status — What is happening?

02. Diagnosis — Why is it happening?

03. Action — What should I do?

04. Forecast — What will happen next?

If users had to work hard to answer these questions, the dashboard was failing its primary purpose.

Working closely with Product, Engineering, Sales, and stakeholders, we explored multiple directions, including enhanced visualizations, investigation workflows, alerts, rule-based recommendations, and AI-assisted decision support.

Rather than adding more reports, we aligned on a solution that could reduce cognitive effort by providing contextual recommendations directly within the existing workflow.

Designing the Experience

Through iterative prototyping and usability testing, I explored how AI could support managers without taking control of their decisions.

Early concepts centered around a conversational assistant. While technically promising, testing showed that users struggled to know what to ask, making the interaction slower than their existing workflow.

I shifted toward contextual insights embedded directly within the dashboard.

Recommendations were prioritized by impact and urgency, AI reasoning was made transparent, and managers always retained full control over the final decision.

Instead of replacing human judgment, the experience was designed to strengthen it.

Results

Because sales performance is influenced by external market conditions, revenue was not used as the primary success metric.

Instead, I measured improvements in decision-making efficiency through a two-week pilot with 24 Sales Managers.

Key outcomes

  • Reduced the average time to identify an issue by 85% (from 34 to 5 minutes)
  • Reduced the average number of interactions per investigation from 12 to 3
  • Increased task success rate from 68% to 95%
  • Average response time reduced from 4 hours to 1.5 hours

The solution was progressively rolled out to ensure realistic validation while minimizing operational risk.

Business Impact

Coach AI was introduced as a premium feature within Spring's platform.

The product was adopted by enterprise customers including Coca-Cola, Red Bull, Unilever, and Hunt Brothers Pizza, creating a new subscription-based revenue stream while expanding the platform's AI capabilities.

Key Learnings

This project reinforced that better decisions don't come from more data, they come from reducing the effort required to interpret it.

Designing AI wasn't about replacing managers' expertise, but providing the right context at the right moment so they could act with greater speed and confidence.
